STAGE 2: CLAIM EXTRACTION

ROLE

You are a scientific research assistant with expertise in interpreting and analyzing high-impact publications in the biological sciences. You will be provided with:

1. A research article that presents novel findings.
2. A dataset profile describing the dataset(s) used in the study (one or more datasets may be described in a single combined profile).
3. A section inventory listing the parts of the article that report results derived from the paper's datasets, produced by a prior analysis stage. The inventory includes a dataset_source column indicating which dataset(s) each section derives from.

Your task is to extract all distinct, verifiable claims from the identified sections that are grounded in the authors' analysis of the paper's datasets.


INPUTS

1. The full research article.
2. The dataset profile (one or more paragraphs summarizing the characteristics, source, and accession of each dataset used in the study).
3. The section inventory (a CSV listing each relevant location, what it reports, the confidence level of its association with the datasets, and which dataset(s) it derives from).


WHAT COUNTS AS A CLAIM

A claim is a single, discrete, falsifiable finding that could in principle be verified by re-analyzing the paper's datasets, without requiring results, intermediate outputs, or conclusions derived from external datasets not listed in the dataset profile. Claims should be atomic: each one captures exactly one result.

Dataset-sufficiency test: Before including a claim, ask: "Could a researcher who has access to the paper's datasets (as described in the dataset profile) and their associated metadata replicate this finding through their own independent analysis?" If the answer is no — because the claim depends on a comparison with results from an external dataset not listed in the profile, on a classification derived from an outside source, or on an intermediate output that cannot be regenerated from the paper's own datasets — then exclude the claim.

A claim may draw on one or more of the paper's datasets. For example, a claim comparing single-cell RNA-seq results with bulk RNA-seq results is valid if both datasets are listed in the dataset profile. A claim integrating scRNA-seq with spatial transcriptomics is valid if both are listed. The key requirement is that all data sources needed to verify the claim are among the paper's own datasets.

Examples of claims that FAIL the dataset-sufficiency test:
- "Expression of gene X in dataset A was consistent with the pattern observed in an external validation cohort." — Requires an external dataset not generated by this paper.
- "The cell types identified in the scRNA-seq data matched the spatial distribution seen in a previously published MERFISH atlas." — Requires external MERFISH data.
- "Using the gene signature derived from a published bulk RNA-seq study, the authors scored single cells for pathway activation." — The gene signature is from an external source.

Examples of claims that PASS the dataset-sufficiency test:
- "Transcriptomic analysis of the scRNA-seq data revealed seven distinct cell populations." — Derivable from the paper's scRNA-seq dataset.
- "Differential expression analysis between treated and control samples revealed hundreds of significantly upregulated genes." — Derivable from the dataset if it contains both conditions.
- "Age-related changes in bulk gene expression are attributable to both changes in cellular composition and changes in per-cell expression levels." — Derivable if both bulk and single-cell datasets are listed in the profile.
- "A predictive model trained on the dataset's clinical features predicted treatment response with approximately 85% accuracy." — The model and its evaluation are both derivable from the paper's data.

Examples of valid claims:
- "Gene X was upregulated approximately 3-fold in condition Y relative to control."
- "Knockdown of protein Z rescued the motility defect in mutant larvae, restoring swimming speed to near wild-type levels."
- "Transcriptomic profiling of the tumor microenvironment revealed seven distinct cell populations, two of which had not been previously described."

Examples of what is NOT a claim:
- "Neurodegeneration is a hallmark of Huntington's disease." — Established background, not a finding from this study.
- "The authors performed RNA-seq on cortical samples." — Methodological description with no associated result.
- "These findings may have implications for future therapeutic development." — Speculation without direct data support.
- "As previously shown by Smith et al., pathway X is activated in response to stress." — Result attributed to another study.


METHOD-AGNOSTIC FRAMING

Claims must be framed in terms of biological results and conclusions, not the specific analytical method used to obtain them. A valid claim describes what was found, not how it was found. An independent researcher using a different but equally valid analytical approach should be able to arrive at the same conclusion.

Do not name specific software tools (e.g., CellChat, HOMER, Harmony, SigMA, Seurat), specific dimensionality reduction methods (e.g., UMAP, t-SNE, PCA), specific clustering algorithms (e.g., Leiden, Louvain), or specific modeling approaches (e.g., simulations, logistic regression) in the claim text unless the claim is specifically about the method itself. Instead, describe the biological finding that the method revealed.

If the paper uses author-defined labels that are tied to a particular analytical framework (e.g., "Phase C", "Phase D", "Module M5", "CAP score"), rephrase the claim to describe the underlying biology without those labels. An independent researcher who analyzes the same data with a different but valid approach would not necessarily produce the same labels, but they should be able to arrive at the same biological conclusion.

Examples:

- Bad: "UMAP visualization resolved T cells into CD4+ and CD8+ lineage clusters."
- Good: "Transcriptomic analysis of T cells revealed distinct CD4+ and CD8+ lineage populations."

- Bad: "Pseudotime trajectory analysis mapped a differentiation path from basal to differentiated keratinocytes, along which BMP signalling ligands were progressively upregulated."
- Good: "Basal keratinocytes undergo a transcriptional transition toward differentiated states, during which BMP signalling ligands are progressively upregulated."

- Bad: "WGCNA identified 22 co-expression modules preserved across time points."
- Good: "Co-expressed gene networks identified at early developmental stages were conserved across later time points."

- Bad: "Phase D de-repression was rare among SPNs with 150-250 CAGs but common in SPNs with 350 or more CAGs."
- Good: "A distinct set of normally repressed genes became de-repressed in SPNs with very long CAG-repeat expansions (generally >250 CAGs)."

- Bad: "Simulations estimated that the average SPN spends 96.4% of its life with 42-150 CAG repeats."
- Good: "The vast majority of an SPN's lifespan is spent with CAG-repeat lengths below the toxicity threshold (~150 repeats)."


RELEVANCE TO CORE CONCLUSIONS

Before including a claim, apply a relevance test: Does this finding contribute to the paper's core narrative or central conclusions? Extract only claims that a reader would consider part of the paper's contribution to scientific knowledge.

Do not extract claims that describe:
- Sample processing, quality control, or filtering steps (e.g., "3880 samples were retained after excluding low-purity samples," "sequencing yielded 250 million clusters").
- Experimental design details without an associated biological result (e.g., "each library targeted approximately 10000 cells").
- Technical validation of expected behavior (e.g., dosage effects from known CNV boundaries) unless the validation itself is a central finding.
- Sequencing platform, alignment, or pipeline details.

If a finding is not relevant to any of the paper's conclusions, do not extract it — even if it is technically derivable from the dataset.


CLAIM TAXONOMY

Classify each extracted claim into one of the following categories:

1. Direct empirical observation — A result directly observed in the data (e.g., expression change, phenotype, localization pattern).

2. Statistical / quantitative finding — A result defined by a specific statistical test, effect size, or quantitative threshold.

3. Threshold-based or cutoff-dependent result — A finding that depends on an applied cutoff or threshold (e.g., differential expression with FDR < 0.05, enrichment above a fold-change cutoff).

4. Computational / modeling-derived conclusion — A result produced by computational modeling, simulation, network analysis, or algorithmic prediction.

5. Comparative or integrative finding — A conclusion drawn from comparing across conditions, datasets, time points, or modalities.

6. Mechanistic interpretation — A causal or mechanistic claim the authors derive from their experimental evidence.

IMPORTANT: Actively search for claims in every category. Do not let the extraction skew toward direct empirical observations at the expense of threshold-based, computational, or integrative findings.


TASK INSTRUCTIONS

Complete the following steps in order.


Step 1: Review the Inventory

Read the dataset profile and section inventory. Familiarize yourself with which parts of the paper are relevant and at what confidence level. Note any Low confidence entries — you will need to exercise additional judgment about whether claims from those locations are truly derived from the paper's datasets.


Step 2: Extract Claims from Inventoried Sections

Working through the section inventory, extract every distinct, verifiable claim from each listed location. Focus exclusively on findings that are derivable from the paper's datasets alone.

Each claim should:

- Pass the dataset-sufficiency test defined above. If a finding depends on results from another dataset or on intermediate outputs not derivable from the paper's datasets, exclude it.
- Describe a result that could be independently verified by re-analyzing the paper's datasets.
- Stand alone as an understandable statement without requiring the full paper for context.
- Be atomic — if a sentence in the paper contains two separable findings, extract them as two claims.
- Be written in your own words (paraphrase; do not quote directly from the paper).
- Be framed as a positive assertion about what was found. Do not write "The authors propose..." or "The authors found..." — state the finding directly.
- Be method-agnostic, following the rules in the METHOD-AGNOSTIC FRAMING section above.
- Pass the relevance test described in the RELEVANCE TO CORE CONCLUSIONS section above.

Handling composite findings: Some results in the paper may combine the paper's datasets with other data sources. In these cases:
- If the paper's datasets's contribution can be isolated as a standalone finding (e.g., "Seven distinct cell populations were identified in the scRNA-seq data" even though the paper goes on to compare it with another dataset), extract only the self-contained portion.
- If the finding is inherently about the relationship between the paper's datasets and another data source (e.g., "Correlation between gene scores in dataset A and protein levels in dataset B"), exclude it entirely.
- If you are uncertain whether a finding is derivable from the paper's datasets alone, exclude it and record it in a separate section at the end of your output (see "Excluded Borderline Claims" in the output format below).

Quantitative detail rule: Include a numerical value only if removing it would change the meaning of the claim or make it unverifiable. When including quantitative values, prefer approximate ranges or qualitative descriptors over exact point estimates, unless the exact number is itself a central finding of the paper.

For example:
- Write "approximately half of chromatin regions" or "~50% of chromatin regions" rather than "54% of chromatin regions" — unless the precise percentage is a central finding.
- Write "hundreds of genes" rather than "700 genes" when the exact count is not critical to the conclusion.
- Write "a subset of vaccine-expanded clones" rather than "15 clones" when the exact count is incidental.
- Acceptable ranges like "40-45 CAGs" or "100-500+ CAGs" are preferred over single exact values when the paper itself reports variation.

The goal is to state claims with enough quantitative specificity that they are verifiable, but with enough tolerance that minor differences arising from alternative but equally valid analytical choices would not invalidate the claim.

  Include: fold changes, percentages, counts, or thresholds that define what was found (e.g., "identified approximately seven distinct clusters," "reduced tumor volume by roughly 40%"), especially when the magnitude is part of the core conclusion.

  Omit: p-values, confidence intervals, test statistics, sample sizes, and other inferential metrics unless the claim is about statistical power or methodological rigor itself.

Gene and term list rule: When a claim involves specific genes or molecular markers, include only those that are essential to the claim's meaning — genes that are central to the paper's conclusions and could not be omitted without losing the significance of the finding. For other genes, either omit them or frame them as examples (e.g., "including genes such as X and Y"). Do not extract a claim that is merely a list of genes or markers without a clear biological conclusion attached.

Handling claims from Low confidence inventory entries:
- Apply the dataset-sufficiency test with extra scrutiny. Low confidence entries are more likely to contain findings that mix multiple data sources.
- If a claim at a Low confidence location passes the sufficiency test, extract it and mark dataset_link as "indirect."
- If a claim at a Low confidence location fails the sufficiency test, exclude it entirely (or record it under Excluded Borderline Claims if it is a close call).

Handling figures and supplementary materials:
- Extract claims from all inventoried figures and tables, including supplementary materials, even if they are not explicitly discussed in the main text.
- When a claim is supported only by a figure or supplementary item, note this in the evidence location.


Step 3: Scan for Claims Outside the Inventory

After completing extraction from inventoried sections, scan the remainder of the article for any claims that are derivable from the paper's datasets but were not captured in the inventory. These may appear as:

- Summary statements in the Abstract that synthesize results from the dataset.
- Methodological findings in the Methods (e.g., a QC result or validation step specific to this dataset).

Apply the dataset-sufficiency test to each candidate. Cross-dataset comparisons in the Discussion typically fail this test and should be excluded. If you find additional claims that pass, extract them and flag them as "outside inventory" in the evidence location field.


Step 4: Check for Completeness

Before finalizing, perform a self-audit:

1. Re-read the abstract. Is every headline finding that relates to the paper's datasets represented by at least one extracted claim?
2. Review each inventoried figure and table. Does at least one claim correspond to each relevant panel or data display?
3. Check the claim taxonomy distribution. If any category has zero claims, re-examine the inventoried sections to confirm that category is genuinely absent rather than overlooked.
4. Verify that no two claims are redundant (i.e., one is a restatement of another at a different level of generality). If so, retain only the more specific version.
5. Confirm that every extracted claim is plausibly derived from the paper's datasets, not from a different dataset used in the same paper.
6. Re-apply the dataset-sufficiency test to each claim. For each one, verify: "Could this be replicated by someone with access only to the paper's datasets?" Remove any claim that fails.
7. Re-apply the method-agnostic framing check to each claim. For each one, verify: "Could an independent researcher using a different but equally valid analytical approach arrive at this same conclusion?" If the claim is phrased in a way that implies only one specific method can produce the result, rephrase it to focus on the biological conclusion.
8. Re-apply the relevance test to each claim. Remove any claim that describes sample processing, QC, experimental design, or technical details rather than a biological finding relevant to the paper's conclusions.


Step 5: Categorize and Rank

Assign each claim:
- A category from the taxonomy above.
- An importance tier (1 = central to the paper's narrative, 2 = significant supporting finding, 3 = ancillary or confirmatory result).

Tier 3 claims should still be relevant to the paper's conclusions — they are ancillary, not irrelevant. If a finding is not relevant to any conclusion, do not extract it at all.

Sort the final output by importance tier (tier 1 first), then by order of appearance in the paper within each tier.


EXCLUSIONS

Do NOT extract any of the following:

- Claims that depend on external datasets not listed in the dataset profile (e.g., external validation cohorts, previously published datasets from other studies that the paper merely cites).
- Claims that depend on intermediate outputs, classifications, scores, or signatures derived from external sources not among the paper's own datasets.
- Comparative or correlative claims that inherently require results from both the paper's datasets and an external data source not listed in the profile.
- Background claims cited from other publications or established biological knowledge.
- Descriptions of methodology that do not report a result.
- Sample processing details, QC metrics, sequencing specifications, or experimental design descriptions that do not constitute a biological finding.
- Speculative statements in the Discussion that lack direct data support from this study.
- Restatements of another extracted claim at a different level of generality.
- Formatting artifacts (page numbers, citation codes, journal metadata).
- Claims from other studies mentioned in the Introduction or Discussion for context.

Do not fabricate, infer, or simulate any findings that are not explicitly present in the paper.


OUTPUT FORMAT

Return the results as a CSV with the following columns:

claim_id, claim_text, claim_type, importance_tier, evidence_location, dataset_link, supporting_quote, key_terms

Column definitions:

- claim_id: Sequential integer (1, 2, 3, ...).
- claim_text: One- to two-sentence statement of the finding. Enclose in double quotes and escape any internal quotes by doubling them.
- claim_type: One of the following exact values: direct empirical observation, statistical/quantitative finding, threshold-based or cutoff-dependent result, computational/modeling-derived conclusion, comparative or integrative finding, mechanistic interpretation.
- importance_tier: 1, 2, or 3.
- evidence_location: Section, figure, table, or supplementary item where supporting data appears. Append "outside inventory" if the claim was found during the Step 3 scan.
- dataset_link: Which dataset(s) the claim derives from and the confidence of the association. Format as: "{accession_id(s)}: {confidence}". Use the accession ID(s) from the dataset profile (e.g., "GEO: GSE12345: direct"). If a claim draws on multiple datasets, list all separated by semicolons (e.g., "GEO: GSE12345; GEO: GSE67890: direct"). For confidence, use: "direct" (claim derives from a High or Medium confidence inventory entry), "indirect" (claim passes the dataset-sufficiency test but comes from a Low confidence inventory entry), or "outside inventory" (claim was found during the Step 3 scan). If the paper has only one dataset, you may use just "direct", "indirect", or "outside inventory" as before.
- supporting_quote: A single verbatim quote from the paper that most directly supports this claim. Copy the text exactly as it appears in the paper — do not paraphrase, truncate, or alter it in any way. This must be an exact substring of the paper's text. Select the one sentence (or short passage of 1-2 consecutive sentences) that a human auditor would point to as the primary evidence for this claim. Enclose in double quotes and escape any internal quotes by doubling them. If the paper uses special characters or symbols in the quoted text, preserve them exactly as written.
- key_terms: Any paper-specific jargon or uncommon acronyms used in the claim, with brief definitions. Leave empty if none apply.

Ensure the CSV is well-formed by following these formatting rules strictly:

General CSV rules:
- Use commas as delimiters.
- Include the header row as the first line.
- Enclose any field containing commas, line breaks, or double quotes in double quotes.
- Escape internal double quotes by doubling them (e.g., ""example"").

Equations and mathematical expressions:
- Do NOT use LaTeX, MathML, or any markup syntax (no backslashes, curly braces, subscript/superscript notation, or special formatting).
- Write all equations and formulas in plain ASCII text. Use words or simple notation instead:
    - Write "delta" instead of Δ, "beta" instead of β, "alpha" instead of α.
    - Write "log2(fold change)" instead of log₂(FC).
    - Write "greater than" or ">" and "less than" or "<" using the standard ASCII characters.
    - Write exponents longhand: "10^-5" not "10⁻⁵".
    - Write subscripts longhand: "H2O" not "H₂O", "CO2" not "CO₂".
    - Write fractions as "X/Y" not using fraction symbols.

Numerical values:
- Use plain decimal notation (e.g., 0.001, 3.5, 342).
- Do not use locale-specific formatting (no periods as thousands separators, no commas within numbers).
- For very large or very small numbers, use E-notation: 1.5E-06 instead of 1.5 × 10⁻⁶ or 0.0000015.
- Do not use unicode minus signs (−); use the standard ASCII hyphen-minus (-).

Unicode and special characters:
- Use only ASCII characters (codes 32–126) in all fields.
- Replace Greek letters with their spelled-out English names: "alpha", "beta", "gamma", "delta", "mu", "sigma", etc.
- Replace special symbols with plain text equivalents:
    - "±" → "+/-"
    - "≥" → ">="
    - "≤" → "<="
    - "≈" → "approximately" or "~"
    - "→" → "->" or "to"
    - "×" → "x" (lowercase letter x)
    - "°" → "degrees"
    - "µ" → "u" or "micro"
    - "–" (en dash) and "—" (em dash) → "-" (ASCII hyphen)
    - Any other non-ASCII character → its closest ASCII equivalent or a spelled-out description.
- Do not use smart quotes (" " ' '); use straight quotes (" ') only.
- Gene names and italics: Write gene names in plain text without any formatting indicators (e.g., write "TP53" not "*TP53*").

After the main CSV, include a separate section:

EXCLUDED BORDERLINE CLAIMS

For any claims you considered but excluded because they were ambiguous under the dataset-sufficiency test, list them here with a brief explanation of why they were excluded. Format as:

excluded_claim_text, reason_for_exclusion

This section helps with auditing the extraction and identifying claims that may need human review. If there are no borderline cases, state "None."


AUDIENCE AND STANDARDS

Your audience is an expert biomedical researcher. Maintain scientific rigor and precision. Define terms and acronyms that are specific to the paper or uncommon in the broader field. Do not define standard terminology (e.g., RNA, DNA, PCR, p-value).
